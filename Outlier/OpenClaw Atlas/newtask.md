🧾 Prompt Idea vs. Technical Brief

It is important to distinguish between the prompt idea and the technical brief shown in the bullet points.

The prompt idea is the main narrative paragraph. It describes the user’s situation, context, goal, uncertainty, and practical challenge in a natural way. It should feel like a realistic request from a person who needs help completing a task.

The technical brief is the structured bullet-point summary that follows the idea. It does not replace the prompt idea. Instead, it highlights the key operational details that may help evaluate or build the final prompt.

For example:

The prompt idea explains the scenario, such as who needs the task, what problem they are facing, why the task matters, and what could go wrong.
The technical brief identifies specific elements like the attached file, expected deliverable, referenced entities, and pressure vector.
🔍 How to Use Both

When analyzing each idea, read the prompt idea first to understand the full scenario. Then use the technical brief to quickly identify the most important details.

As you evaluate the 5 ideas, consider both parts:

🔹 Does the prompt idea present a realistic and useful task?
🔹 Does the technical brief accurately summarize the task?
🔹 Are the bullet points consistent with the narrative paragraph?
🔹 Do the listed entities, deliverable, and pressure vector help clarify the final prompt?
🔹 Is there any important detail in the prompt idea that is missing from the technical brief?
🔹 Is there any bullet-point detail that does not clearly appear in the prompt idea?

The final prompt should be based on the substance of the prompt idea, while using the technical brief as a support tool for clarity, consistency, and completeness.

⚖️ Desired Outcome vs. Effective Execution

The Desired Outcome serves strictly as the blueprint or plan for the trajectory. However, it cannot be treated as the source of truth for the entire task. When executing the rest of the task—specifically the rubrics and tests—what is effectively used and executed within the actual trajectory is what absolutely rules 👑.

🎯 Why it's critical

🧱 A plan, not a law — the Desired Outcome only defines the intended path for the trajectory, nothing more.

🧪 Rules rubrics and tests — evaluation metrics and test cases must be derived exclusively from what is effectively used in the conversation.

🗣️ Effective primacy — what you actually see and use within the trajectory always overrides any pre-planned outcome.

🚫 Prevents ghost grading — ensures you don't test or grade requirements that were planned but never actually manifested in the real interaction.

🔗 Coherence between plan and evaluation

The Desired Outcome and your evaluation tools govern two entirely different dimensions of the task:

🎯 The Desired Outcome = the planned target for the trajectory.

📝 The Actual Interaction = the evaluation of what is effectively used inside the conversation.

The plan sets the stage, but the actual execution writes the rules for the rubrics and tests.
