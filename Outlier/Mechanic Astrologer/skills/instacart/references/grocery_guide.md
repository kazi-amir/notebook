# Smart Grocery Shopping Guide

Reference guide for interpreting Instacart grocery data and providing shopping advice.

## Spending Benchmarks

### Weekly/Monthly Averages (US, 2025)

| Household Size | Weekly | Monthly |
|---------------|--------|---------|
| 1 person | $60–$90 | $250–$375 |
| 2 people | $100–$150 | $400–$625 |
| 3–4 people | $150–$250 | $625–$1,000 |
| 5+ people | $200–$350 | $800–$1,400 |

These include delivery fees and tips typical of Instacart. Costs vary by region — metro areas trend higher.

### How to Spot Overspending

- Monthly grocery spend exceeding 15% of take-home income
- Average order total rising month-over-month without household changes
- High Snacks/Beverages/Frozen spend relative to Produce/Protein
- Duplicate items across orders (buying before using up previous purchase)

## Category Balance

A well-balanced grocery cart by spend allocation:

| Category | Target % | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Produce | 25–35% | Fruits, vegetables, fresh herbs |
| Meat & Seafood | 20–25% | Protein sources |
| Dairy | 10–15% | Milk, cheese, yogurt, eggs |
| Pantry & Staples | 10–15% | Grains, pasta, canned goods, oils |
| Bakery & Breakfast | 5–10% | Bread, cereal, oatmeal |
| Beverages | 5–8% | Water, juice, coffee, tea |
| Snacks | 3–5% | Chips, crackers, bars |
| Frozen | 3–5% | Frozen meals, vegetables, treats |
| Household | 5–10% | Cleaning, paper goods |

### Red Flags

- Produce < 15% — may indicate too few fruits/vegetables
- Snacks + Frozen > 20% — processed food dominance
- Beverages > 15% — sugary drinks or premium coffee adding up
- No variety in protein sources — consider rotating chicken, fish, beans

## Seasonal Produce Guide

Buying in-season produce saves money and improves quality.

### Spring (March–May)
Asparagus, artichokes, peas, strawberries, radishes, spinach, rhubarb

### Summer (June–August)
Tomatoes, corn, peaches, berries (blueberries, raspberries), watermelon, zucchini, bell peppers, cucumbers, cherries

### Fall (September–November)
Apples, pears, pumpkin, sweet potatoes, cranberries, Brussels sprouts, squash (butternut, acorn), grapes

### Winter (December–February)
Citrus (oranges, grapefruit, clementines), kale, root vegetables (carrots, turnips, beets), pomegranates, cabbage

## Budget Tips

### Bulk Buying

Best for non-perishables with high per-unit savings:
- Rice, pasta, oats, flour
- Canned goods (beans, tomatoes)
- Cooking oils, vinegar
- Paper towels, toilet paper
- Frozen vegetables and fruits

Avoid bulk buying for perishables unless you can use them before expiration.

### Organic vs. Conventional

The "Dirty Dozen" — worth buying organic (high pesticide residue):
- Strawberries, spinach, kale, peaches, pears, nectarines, apples, grapes, bell peppers, cherries, blueberries, green beans

The "Clean Fifteen" — conventional is fine (low pesticide residue):
- Avocados, sweet corn, pineapple, onions, papaya, frozen peas, asparagus, honeydew, kiwi, cabbage, mushrooms, mangoes, sweet potatoes, watermelon, carrots

### Unit Price Comparison

When comparing products:
- Convert all prices to same unit (per oz, per lb, per count)
- Larger sizes are not always cheaper per unit
- Store brands average 20–30% less than name brands for equivalent quality
- Check variant prices — a 16 oz container at $7.99 vs 5 oz at $4.99 = $0.50/oz vs $1.00/oz

## Shopping Frequency Patterns

| Pattern | Frequency | Characteristics |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Stock-up shopper | Every 1–2 weeks | Large orders ($100+), bulk items, meal planning |
| Frequent shopper | 2–3x per week | Smaller orders ($30–$60), fresh focus |
| As-needed shopper | Varies | Triggered by running out of items |
| Meal-kit style | Weekly | Recipe-based shopping, precise quantities |

### Optimizing Order Frequency

- **Fewer, larger orders** reduce delivery fees but risk food waste
- **More frequent, smaller orders** keep produce fresher but cost more in fees
- **Sweet spot**: Weekly orders with a mid-week top-up for perishables
- **Instacart+** membership ($99/year) makes frequent ordering more economical with free delivery on $35+ orders

## Interpreting Favorites

When analyzing a user's frequently purchased items:

- **Staples** (bought every order): milk, eggs, bread, bananas — these form the shopping baseline
- **Regulars** (bought most orders): specific proteins, favorite snacks — these define preferences
- **Occasionals** (bought sometimes): seasonal items, recipe-specific — these show variety

If favorites are dominated by staples with few regulars, the user may benefit from exploring new products. If favorites show high variety, the user is an adventurous shopper.
