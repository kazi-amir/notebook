# Body Composition Analysis Guide

Use this guide to interpret Renpho smart scale metrics and provide science-backed insights.

## 5-Step Analysis Framework

1. **Status**: Current state of each metric (healthy/borderline/concerning)
2. **Trends**: Direction over time (improving/stable/declining)
3. **Patterns**: Weekly weigh-in variability, plateau detection
4. **Insights**: Actionable takeaways based on the data
5. **Flags**: Red flags that suggest medical consultation

Always include a disclaimer: "This analysis is for informational purposes only and is not medical advice."

---

## Weight & BMI

### BMI Classifications

| BMI | Classification | Notes |
|-----|---------------|-------|
| <18.5 | Underweight | May indicate nutritional deficiency |
| 18.5-24.9 | Normal | Healthy weight range |
| 25.0-29.9 | Overweight | Increased health risk |
| 30.0-34.9 | Obese Class I | High health risk |
| 35.0-39.9 | Obese Class II | Very high health risk |
| 40.0+ | Obese Class III | Extremely high health risk |

### BMI Limitations
- Does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass
- Athletes may have high BMI with healthy body composition
- Does not account for age, gender, or frame size
- Always interpret alongside body fat % and muscle mass

### Weight Trend Interpretation
- **Losing >1 kg/week**: Rapid loss — verify adequate nutrition
- **Losing 0.25-1 kg/week**: Healthy rate for weight loss goals
- **Stable (±0.5 kg)**: Maintenance phase
- **Gaining 0.25-0.5 kg/week**: Could be muscle gain if training, or gradual fat gain
- **Gaining >1 kg/week**: Rapid gain — likely water retention or dietary change

### Daily Weight Fluctuations
Normal daily fluctuation is 0.5-2 kg due to:
- Hydration levels
- Food/sodium intake
- Time of day
- Hormonal cycles
- Bowel movements

Best practice: Weigh at the same time daily (morning, after bathroom, before eating). Compare weekly averages, not daily readings.

### Healthy Ranges by Gender

| Category | Male | Female |
|----------|------|--------|
| Essential Fat | 2-5% | 10-13% |
| Athletes | 6-13% | 14-20% |
| Fitness | 14-17% | 21-24% |
| Average | 18-24% | 25-31% |
| Obese | 25%+ | 32%+ |

### Age-Adjusted Body Fat (Healthy Ranges)

| Age | Male | Female |
|-----|------|--------|
| 20-29 | 10-20% | 18-28% |
| 30-39 | 12-22% | 20-30% |
| 40-49 | 14-24% | 22-32% |
| 50-59 | 16-25% | 24-33% |
| 60+ | 17-26% | 25-35% |

### Body Fat Trends
- **Decreasing**: Good if currently above healthy range
- **Stable**: Good if in healthy range
- **Increasing**: Concerning if already in average/obese range
- **Rapid decrease** (>1%/week): May indicate muscle loss — check muscle mass trend

## Muscle Mass

### Typical Ranges

| Gender | Percentage of Body Weight |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Male | 33-40% |
| Female | 25-33% |

### Interpreting Muscle Mass Changes
- **Increasing with stable/decreasing weight**: Excellent — body recomposition
- **Decreasing with weight loss**: May indicate inadequate protein or too-aggressive caloric deficit
- **Stable with weight gain**: Likely fat gain
- **Increasing with weight gain**: Could be healthy muscle building

### Factors Affecting Muscle Mass
- Resistance training (primary driver of muscle growth)
- Protein intake (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day for muscle building)
- Sleep quality (growth hormone release)
- Age (sarcopenia begins ~30, accelerates after 50)
- Hormonal status

## Visceral Fat

Visceral fat surrounds internal organs and is a key health risk indicator.

| Level | Rating | Health Risk |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 1-9 | Healthy | Normal |
| 10-14 | Elevated | Increased risk of metabolic syndrome |
| 15+ | High | Significantly increased cardiovascular and metabolic risk |

### Visceral Fat vs. Body Fat
- Possible to have normal body fat % but high visceral fat
- Visceral fat is more metabolically active and inflammatory
- Responds well to aerobic exercise and dietary changes
- Strongly correlated with insulin resistance

## Water Percentage

### Healthy Ranges

| Gender | Range |
|--------|-------|
| Male | 50-65% |
| Female | 45-60% |

### Interpretation
- **Low**: May indicate dehydration, high body fat ratio
- **Normal**: Good hydration status
- **High**: Can indicate recent fluid intake, low body fat
- Athletes tend to have higher water percentage due to lower fat

### Factors Affecting Readings
- Time of day (lower in morning due to overnight dehydration)
- Recent exercise (temporary decrease from sweating)
- Sodium intake (temporary water retention)
- Alcohol (dehydrating effect)
- Menstrual cycle (water retention in luteal phase)

## Metabolic Age

Compares your BMR to the average BMR for different age groups.

### Interpretation
- **Below actual age**: Better-than-average metabolic health
- **Equal to actual age**: Average metabolic health
- **Above actual age**: Below-average metabolic health — room for improvement

### Improving Metabolic Age
- Build muscle mass (increases BMR)
- Reduce body fat (especially visceral)
- Regular exercise (both cardio and strength)
- Adequate sleep and stress management

---

## BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)

The calories your body needs at complete rest.

### Rough Guidelines

| Activity Level | Daily Calories |
|---------------|----------------|
| Sedentary | BMR × 1.2 |
| Lightly Active | BMR × 1.375 |
| Moderately Active | BMR × 1.55 |
| Very Active | BMR × 1.725 |
| Extremely Active | BMR × 1.9 |

## Pattern Detection

### Look For
1. **Plateau**: Weight stable >3 weeks despite caloric deficit — may need diet/exercise adjustment
2. **Yo-yo pattern**: Repeated loss/gain cycles — focus on sustainable changes
3. **Muscle loss with weight loss**: Increase protein, add resistance training
4. **Rising visceral fat**: Even with stable weight — increase aerobic exercise
5. **Dehydration trend**: Consistently low water % — increase fluid intake

### Red Flags (Recommend Medical Consultation)
- Unintentional weight loss >5% in 6 months
- BMI <16 or >40
- Visceral fat consistently >15
- Metabolic age >15 years above actual age
- Sudden significant changes in any metric without lifestyle explanation

## Actionable Recommendations

### For Weight Loss
1. Target 0.5-1 kg/week (500-1000 kcal daily deficit)
2. Prioritize protein (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day) to preserve muscle
3. Include resistance training 2-3x/week
4. Track trends over weeks, not daily fluctuations

### For Muscle Building
1. Small caloric surplus (200-500 kcal/day)
2. High protein intake (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day)
3. Progressive overload in resistance training
4. Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)

### For Reducing Visceral Fat
1. Regular aerobic exercise (150+ min/week moderate intensity)
2. Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars
3. Manage stress (cortisol promotes visceral fat storage)
4. Prioritize sleep quality

### For Improving Body Composition
1. Focus on body fat % and muscle mass trends, not just weight
2. Combine resistance and cardiovascular training
3. Adequate protein and balanced nutrition
4. Consistent measurement timing for accurate tracking
